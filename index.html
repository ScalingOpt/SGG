Scaling Laws for Gaussian Random Minima Flattening

Understanding the scaling behavior of Gaussian random minima flattening in high-dimensional optimization landscapes

arXiv Paper GitHub Code

Abstract

We investigate the scaling behavior of Gaussian random minima flattening (GRMF) in high-dimensional non-convex optimization landscapes. Our analysis reveals universal power-law scaling relationships between the flattening effectiveness and key parameters including dimensionality, noise magnitude, and curvature statistics. We derive theoretical bounds for the expected reduction in loss function barriers and verify them through extensive numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world optimization problems.

The key findings include: (1) a dimensionality scaling exponent of -1/2 for barrier reduction, (2) a noise magnitude scaling exponent of 3/2, and (3) curvature-dependent scaling that transitions between two distinct regimes. These scaling laws provide practical guidelines for parameter selection in optimization algorithms employing GRMF techniques.

Introduction

Background and Motivation

Non-convex optimization landscapes are characterized by numerous local minima, saddle points, and complex barrier structures that pose significant challenges for optimization algorithms. Gaussian random minima flattening (GRMF) has emerged as a promising technique to mitigate these challenges by systematically reducing the height of barriers between minima.

Despite its empirical success, the theoretical understanding of GRMF's scaling behavior with respect to problem dimensionality and landscape characteristics remains incomplete. This work fills that gap by deriving and validating scaling laws that govern the effectiveness of GRMF across different parameter regimes.

Figure 1: Illustration of Gaussian random minima flattening in a 2D optimization landscape. Left: Original landscape. Right: After GRMF application showing reduced barrier heights.

Key Contributions

Our main contributions are:

  1. Derivation of scaling laws for GRMF effectiveness as a function of dimensionality, noise magnitude, and curvature statistics
  2. Identification of two distinct scaling regimes based on the relationship between noise magnitude and curvature
  3. Empirical validation across synthetic landscapes and real-world optimization problems
  4. Practical guidelines for parameter selection in GRMF-enhanced optimization algorithms

Key Results

Dimensionality Scaling

We establish that the expected barrier reduction ΔE scales with dimensionality d as:

ΔE ∼ σ3/2 d-1/2 κ-1

where σ is the noise magnitude and κ is the characteristic curvature.

100 0.100 0.097 ± 0.006 3.0%

Noise Magnitude Scaling

The scaling with noise magnitude σ follows a 3/2 power law, as predicted by our theoretical analysis. This relationship holds across different curvature regimes, though the prefactor changes based on the κ/σ ratio.

Figure 2: Log-log plot of barrier reduction versus noise magnitude showing the 3/2 power law scaling. Different colors represent different curvature regimes.

Curvature-Dependent Scaling

We identify two distinct scaling regimes based on the ratio of noise magnitude to curvature (σ/κ):

  1. Weak noise regime (σ/κ ≪ 1): Linear scaling with σ
  2. Strong noise regime (σ/κ ≫ 1): 3/2 power law scaling with σ

Transition 1.25 ± 0.05 - 0.3 ≤ σ/κ ≤ 2.0
Strong noise 1.51 ± 0.04 σ/κ ≈ 2.0 σ/κ > 2.0

Method

Gaussian Random Minima Flattening

The GRMF procedure consists of the following steps:

  1. Identify local minima xi in the original landscape
  2. Generate Gaussian random perturbations δ ∼ N(0, σ2I)
  3. Construct the modified potential: V'(x) = V(x) + ∑i φ(|x - xi|/λ) δi
  4. Optimize the smoothing length λ to maximize barrier reduction

Theoretical Analysis

We model the barrier reduction using random matrix theory and extreme value statistics. The key steps in our derivation include:

Conclusion

We have established fundamental scaling laws for Gaussian random minima flattening in high-dimensional optimization landscapes. The derived relationships between barrier reduction and key parameters (dimensionality, noise magnitude, and curvature) provide both theoretical insights and practical guidelines for algorithm design.

Key implications of our work include:

  • Optimal noise scaling should follow σ ∼ d1/3 to maintain constant expected barrier reduction
  • The identified scaling regimes suggest adaptive noise strategies based on local curvature estimates
  • The dimensional scaling law explains the effectiveness of GRMF in high-dimensional problems
